Intellectual Property: Global Protection and Legal Implications

Noah M. Jacobson
Herzing University
Computer Security
Professor Schleckser
December 10, 2023

Abstract

This paper delves into the concept of intellectual Property (IP) and its legal protection in various countries, focusing on the United States and Europe. It explores the definition of IP, its legal frameworks, including the Fair Use Act, Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property (TRIPS), Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA), and European Union Directive 95/46/EC. Additionally, it discusses legislation like the Sarbanes Oxley Act of 2002, the USA PATRIOT ACT, Financial Services Modernization Act (Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act of 1999), and HIPAA ACT of 1996. Finally, it addresses whether IP enjoys uniform protection across all nations.

Intellectual Property: Global Protection and Legal Implications

The definition of intellectual property refers to the creation of the mind-such as inventions, literary or artistic works, designs, symbols, names, and images- used in commerce. It encompasses patents, copyrights, trademarks, and trade secrets, offering legal protection against unauthorized use. However, the level and type of protection vary across countries.
The Fair Use Act, which is a doctrine under the US Copyright Law, states that “the statutory framework for determining whether something is a fair use and identifies certain types of uses-such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, and research-as examples of activities that may qualify as fair use.” (2023). This act limits the use of copyrighted material without permission for these purposes. It’s a crucial provision that balances the rights of copyright holders and public access to information.
The Trade-related aspests of intellectual property (TRIPS) was established by the World Trade Organization (WTO), and sets international standards for IP protections, encouraging member counties to adhere to minimum IP standards, including patents, copyrights, and trademarks, fostering fair competition and protection of innovation globally. The TRIPS agreement also sets standards for the enforcement of intellectual property rights (IPRs) “through civil actions for infringement, actions at the border and, at least in regard to copyright piracy and trademark counterfeiting, in criminal actions.” (USTR, n.d.). This is important for anyone that yields significant benefits, whether industries or individuals, as it protects the interests of the U.S.
The DMCA addresses issues related to digital copyright infringement and circumvention of technological protection measures. It aligns with the World Intellectual Property Organization's (WIPO) efforts to harmonize IP rights internationally, ensuring creators and rights holders are safeguarded in the digital age. This act addresses copyright issues that were created with the use of new technology, the internet, and digital rights management, and certain rights/ privileges to protect internet service providers. It combined four proposed bills along with some other revisions to copyright laws to fill in gaps within the WIPO copyright treaty (Wex, 2022).
Directive 95/46/EC, adopted by the European Union, regulates the processing of personal data within the EU member states, emphasizing individuals' rights regarding their personal information. It enhances privacy protection, impacting IP-related data handling within the EU. The European Union seeked to harmonize the protection of fundamental rights and feedoms of natural persons in respect of processing activities and to ensure the free flow of personal data between Member States. (Wikipedia, 2023)
The Sarbanes Oxley Act of 2002, primarily focusing on financial reporting and corporate governance, includes the 'Due Care' clause, emphasizing companies' responsibility to handle financial information with due diligence, indirectly affecting the protection of financial IP. This came as a response to multiple financial scandals in the early 2000s that involved public trading companies. These fraudulent acts shook investors' confidence in corporate financial statements which led to an overhaul of decades-old regulatory standards (Kenton, 2022).
The USA PATRIOT ACT, enacted for national security purposes, extends the government's surveillance and investigation powers, potentially impacting data privacy and confidentiality, thereby influencing aspects of IP protection on a global scale. This act helps law enforcement to identify, dismantle, disrupt, and punish terrorists before they strike. “This equips federal law enforcement and intelligence officials with the tools they need to mount and effective, coordinated campaign against our nation's terrorist enemies.” (U.S Department of Justice, 2004).
The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act of 1999 facilitates mergers between financial institutions, impacting data sharing and confidentiality. It requires “companies that offer consumers financial products or services to explain their information and sharing practices to their customers and to safeguard sensitive data.” (Federal Trade Commission, n.d.). The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996 ensures the security and privacy of health data, affecting the protection of medical-related IP. This is a federal law that requires the creation of national standards to protect a patient’s sensitive health information from being disclosed without the permission and knowledge of the patient (CDC, 2022).
Unfortunately, IP isn’t uniformly protected globally. While international agreements like TRIPS strive for harmonization, differences in legal systems, enforcement capabilities, and cultural perceptions affect the level and effectiveness of IP protection. The laws in the United States and Europe illustrate variations in approaches and scopes of protections, further highlighting the lack of uniformity in IP safeguarding across countries.
In conclusion, Intellectual Property, a cornerstone of innovation and creativity, faces diverse legal landscapes worldwide. While efforts like TRIPS and regional directives aim to standardize protection, differences in laws, enforcement mechanisms, and cultural contexts result in varying levels of safeguarding IP. The United States and Europe exemplify this divergence, emphasizing the need for continued international cooperations to enhance global IP protection.
